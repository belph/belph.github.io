Why Study Computer Science?

While it may seem to be common knowledge that in today’s digital era a degree in computer science is an economically viable option, little focus has been placed on what the discipline is actually all about.

If you or someone you know is on the fence about whether or not to set out to study computer science, this will hopefully shed some light on what you or they would be getting into.

Computer Science is a Misnomer

Contrary to what the name implies, computer science is not all about computers. While, yes, students may learn how to program during their course of study, that is not the focus; instead, most computer science curricula impart an understanding of computation unto their students.

While this may sound like mere pedantry, the reality is that there is a real distinction between the two concepts. The gist is that former is simply a machine which performs the latter. Computer scientists must, of course, understand how to make computers do what they want, but their primary area of interest instead stems from what it is they are asking the computer to do.

What Universities Have to Say

One natural place to look for ways of understanding the nature of computer science is any of the university website out there advertising their own academic programs and colleges. While it is important to bear in mind that these pages are geared towards ‘selling’ the university to prospective students, they nevertheless provide some insight into the various ways this one area of study can be portrayed.

Northeastern University’s Learning Outcomes page (which describes what the college aims to teach its students) has a number of points which are entirely objective until the last few, which involve “be[ing] sensitive to the relationship of technology to social issues,” “show[ing] respect for the diversity of all individuals [in computing],” and “communication within organizations and to the public [being] essential.”

The vocabulary choices for these bullet points indicate the college has placed significant value in these notions. This is curious, however, for ‘relating to society’ is not something that immediately jumps to mind when pondering computer science’s nature. Nevertheless, this is the first of many references to this notion of the importance of the discipline in areas beyond its dictionary definition.

Harvard College also underscores this notion through their emphases on the importance of computer science majors receiving a liberal-arts-oriented education. In addition to the standard incentive of well-roundedness, this curriculum style is intended for students to be able to integrate into various sectors in society.

“Therein lies the excitement. CS empowers you with tools and ideas that can be applied to practically any domain of interest.” This statement from Harvard’s “Getting Started” site sums up the page’s overall theme of computer science’s value being largely drawn from the fact that its domain knowledge can be applied to a variety of contexts. Furthermore, this value is complemented by the fact that, in addition to being able to apply this knowledge, there is a strong need for this expertise to be utilized in a broad spectrum of fields.

While Harvard shines the light on where the skills in computer science can be applied, Carnegie Mellon University seems to focus more on the skills themselves. They conclude their program description page’s introductory paragraph with an assertion:

"[Y]ou’re guaranteed to find a rigorous program dedicated to the real-world training and practical problem solving that has been the hallmark of computer science education of CMU since its inception."

This describes a perspective in which the ability to apply the skills learned in computer science in a practical setting is paramount, evidenced by their claim to be “dedicated” to such training.

The perspective on the good of computer science given by Caltech is similar to that of Carnegie Mellon: the overarching theme is the importance of the ability to “tackle challenging and fundamental problems with the potential for long-term and real-world impact,” to quote their website.

So, What is Computer Science All About?

In addition to the standard definition of “computer science is the study of computation,” these four universities collectively shine light on some of the more subtle aspects of the field.

Most significantly, Harvard underscores the interdisciplinary nature of many tasks in computer science. While Carnegie Mellon, Caltech, and Northeastern instead focus more on the skills applied in such tasks, their emphases on collaboration and communication nevertheless help validate the importance of such multidisciplinary tasks faced by computer scientists in the ‘real world.’

Caltech asserts that “[c]omputation is now pervasive, wide-reaching, and unavoidable in our daily lives.” Thus, even a broad-sweeping definition of ‘the problem-solvers of tomorrow’ cannot capture the essence of computer science without some additional consideration for the growing role those who study it are playing in society.

Gone are the days when professional programmers worked solely for IBM and Xerox; as computers become more and more prevalent in our lives, the significance of the field of computer science has skyrocketed to a point at which it is now a critical sector of our society. In short, computer science is not just about fixing tomorrow’s problems. It’s about making tomorrow work.
